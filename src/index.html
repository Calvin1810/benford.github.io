by CDTs Gautum, Lu, and Rao


Benford Analysis of six datasets to determine fraudulent activity.


Benford's Law states the first digit of numbers in some data sets is governed by ${tex`p_i = \log_{10}\left (1 + \frac{1}{i}\right)`} where ${tex`p_i`} is the probability of the digit ${tex`i \in [1...9]`}. The following table shows the expected probability for each digit. | ${tex`i`} | ${tex`p_i`} | |---|----------| | 1 | 0.30103 | | 2 | 0.17609 | | 3 | 0.12494 | | 4 | 0.09691 | | 5 | 0.079181 | | 6 | 0.066947 | | 7 | 0.057992 | | 8 | 0.051153 | | 9 | 0.045757 | This "Law" is common enough that Benford's Law has been used to test for accounting fraud, government corruption, and the validity of scientific results, such as COVID-19 data. Not all data have this distribution, for Benford's law to hold data should - **have sufficient span** either a complete or several orders of magnitude in the data's span. - **have large enough sample size** random chance could make a few first digits non-Benford. If the data set is small enough (say 25 or less), this could have a significant impact on statistical tests for Benford distributions. - **be right skewed** often this is associated with some combinatory or self-organized aspect of the data. Right skew generally increases the span, returning us to the first expectation. - **not be human assigned** serial numbers or telephone numbers would not work.
